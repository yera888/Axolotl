What is an axolotl?

An axolotl is a neotenic salamander. Instead of completing full metamorphosis like most salamanders do, axolotls remain fully aquatic and retain their feathery external gills, broad smile, and finned tail into adulthood. They’re native to the canals and wetlands of Xochimilco in Mexico City, where they once thrived but are now critically endangered in the wild due to habitat loss, pollution, and invasive species. Axolotls are famous for their extraordinary ability to regenerate. They can regrow limbs, spinal cord, parts of the heart and brain, and even sections of their jaws without scarring. In care, they prefer cool, clean, gently moving freshwater, low light, and a protein-rich diet liek earthworms. Adults typically reach 9–12 inches (23–30 cm). Their colors or morphs range from wild-type olive and speckled to leucistic, melanoid, albino, golden, and even GFP lines that fluoresce under UV.

What are Salamanders and metamorphosis

Salamanders are amphibians that, like frogs and newts, start off living in the water. They usually go through metamorphosis to become adults like a tadpole transforming into a frog and then mostly live on land.

  • They replace water breathing gills with air breathing lungs.
  • They grow eyelids and adapt their bodies for land.

Why Axolotls are different...

Axolotls never make this transition. They keep their frilly external gills and other juvenile features and remain aquatic for their entire lifecycle this is called neoteny (or paedomorphism). Although axolotls look like the “tadpole” form of most salamanders, they do become adults in the sense that they can breed. They develop some lung tissue and can also breathe through their skin.

Close relatives?

Axolotls’ closest relatives are tiger salamanders (Ambystoma tigrinum). As a group, Ambystoma salamanders are called mole salamanders for the land dwelling adults’ habit of living underground.
