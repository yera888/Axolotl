What is an axolotl?

The axolotl (Ambystoma mexicanum) is a type of salamander that doesn’t go through metamorphosis.

Salamanders & metamorphosis

Salamanders are amphibians that, like frogs and newts, start off living in the water. Salamanders usually go through a process called metamorphosis to become adults—like a tadpole transforming into a frog—and then mostly live on land.

  • They replace water-breathing gills with air-breathing lungs.
  • They grow eyelids and adapt for life on land.

Axolotls are different

Axolotls never make this transition. They keep frilly external gills and other juvenile features and stay in the water for their entire lifecycle. This is called neoteny, or paedomorphism.

Although axolotls look like the “tadpole” form of most salamanders, they do become adults in the sense that they can breed. They also develop some lung tissue, and breathe through their skin.

Close relatives

Their closest relatives are tiger salamanders (Ambystoma tigrinum). As a group, Ambystoma salamanders are known as mole salamanders for the land-dwelling adults’ habit of living underground.
